Cosmological Timeline

A lot of the events in the Hot Big Bang take place in stages which overlap with each other. Making this graph gave me a better perspective on when it all happened, as well as giving me some practice with annotations, shading, solving the Saha equation, and doing axis breaks in Matplotlib (spoiler alert: it's messy!)

Transit Light Curve

This was a nice opportunity to get my teeth into some real data -- more specifically, data from the Kepler mission. This particular light curve is of WASP-47b, a Hot Jupiter orbiting WASP-47, a Sun-like star 870 light years away in Aquarius. A lot of information can be gleamed from the information annotated in this light curve, such as the planet's mass, radius, and orbital semi-major axis.

Ising Model

This is a plot of the magnetisation of a lattice of interacting spins in a magnetic field as a function of temperature, according to the Ising model. This involved solving lots of messy transcendental equations numerically, but the result has a strange beauty to it. The different lines correspond to different magnetic field strengths, and I was particularly pleased with the colour scheme, which took some messing around with Matplotlib's colormaps.

Van der Waals Isotherms

This one was rather easier to make, as it just involves plotting a not-too- difficult function. The hardest part was converting between the Chemist-aligned units and the SI -- litres squared bars per mole anyone?? In fact, these isotherms have a crucial part missing from the actual behaviour -- see the Maxwell Construction image.

Maxwell Constructions

This is a zoomed-in version of the plot of van der Waals isotherms, but now featuring Maxwell constructions to correct for the non-physical behaviour in the middle. Maxwell constructions are horizontal lines drawn so that the area between the na√Øve isotherms and said horizontal line is net zero (equal amounts above and below). As might be imagined, this involves some somewhat heavy numerical work, but it looks quite nice in the end.

But Matplotlib can't do everything. Below you'll find a plot I made using a more advanced library: Bokeh. The plot shows the results of applying an unsupervised neural network I trained with images of 6,690 objects detected by the Dark Energy Survey, in 5 wavelength bands; you can see these images by hovering over the points (though the tooltip functionality is a little dodgy). Not only can you see that the network has rather effectively separated the "stars" from the "galaxies" (point sources vs extended sources), you can see for yourself that the network has spotted several other features in the data as well, particularly regarding the high-redshift quasars (the crosses)...
